% ABSTRACT
% ===================================================================
\begin{abstract}
Image preprocessing is a near-universal step in medical image classification pipelines, yet its impact on model accuracy remains insufficiently characterized, particularly with respect to the ordering of multi-transform compositions. This study reports an exhaustive combinatorial evaluation of all 65 pipelines constructible from four fundamental transforms---histogram equalization, intensity normalization, non-local means denoising, and color space conversion---applied to a four-block convolutional neural network trained on the binary healthy-versus-diseased dermatoscopic classification task formed by using a dataset with 20{,}000 balanced images. Every pipeline was trained from scratch under five independent random seeds. Against a near-ceiling baseline of 98.09\% test accuracy, which arithmetically bounds the detectable positive-$\alpha$ regime to at most $+1.91$\,pp, no pipeline of length 2 or greater achieves a positive mean accuracy gain, and only equalization alone produces a seed-robust improvement. Holm-corrected permutation tests establish three ordering regularities: pipeline length correlates negatively with accuracy gain ($r = -0.44$), equalization-first placement outperforms alternative first-position assignments by $1.10$ pp (Cohen's $d = 1.22$ against the pooled not-first control; $d = 1.39$ and $\Delta = +1.22$\,pp against the within-inclusion not-first control that excludes equalization-absent pipelines), and the bookend configuration with equalization first and normalization last outperforms its mirror image by $2.00$ pp (Cohen's $d = 2.38$). At pipeline $k=3$, ordering accounts for 58\% of accuracy-gain variance and transform selection for 42\%. Confusion-matrix decomposition indicates that preprocessing-induced degradation is dominated by bidirectional feature-space damage (78\%) rather than recoverable decision-threshold shift (22\%). Within the scope of this single network, single binary task, single dataset, and single transform set, and conditioned on the ceiling-bounded regime evaluated here, the evidence supports a cautious harm-minimization heuristic---equalization first, normalization last---rather than a general claim about ordering dominance in medical image preprocessing.
\end{abstract}

% I. INTRODUCTION

\section{Introduction}
The field of dermatology has experienced rapid adoption of artificial intelligence tools for diagnostic support, particularly through computer-aided diagnosis (CADx) systems trained on dermatoscopic and clinical images~\cite{Krakowski2024}. Deep learning models can increase diagnostic accuracy for skin diseases by up to 12 percentage points (pp) relative to unaided clinical assessment~\cite{Esteva2017, 10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2021.7249, Brinker2019}, a substantial margin given the 66.3\% baseline accuracy reported for human physicians in certain diagnostic tasks~\cite{Barnett2019}. These performance gains, however, depend critically on the quality and preparation of the input images fed to the classification model~\cite{ALHINAI20201, Brinker2019}.

Image preprocessing---the application of transforms such as normalization, histogram equalization, denoising, and color space conversion prior to model ingestion---is widely regarded as a necessary step for achieving reliable classification performance~\cite{RODRIGUES2020103542, 9548351, Tarawneh2024}. In practice, preprocessing pipelines are typically constructed by selecting a set of transforms believed to be individually beneficial and applying them in an order motivated by domain convention or intuition. A typical workflow might normalize intensity values, apply noise reduction, and enhance contrast, with the ordering dictated by the practitioner's understanding of each transform's purpose rather than by systematic evaluation.

This conventional approach implicitly assumes two properties of preprocessing pipelines. First, it assumes that the choice of which transforms to include (transform selection) is the primary determinant of pipeline performance. Second, it assumes that the order in which selected transforms are applied (transform ordering) is either irrelevant or can be determined from first principles without empirical evaluation. Neither assumption has been rigorously assessed in this context. Because common preprocessing transforms---histogram equalization, nonlinear denoising, color space conversion---are nonlinear and data-dependent, the mathematical non-commutativity of their composition is expected in principle but has not been quantified in terms of its impact on downstream classification accuracy.

This paper aims to address this gap through an exhaustive combinatorial evaluation of all possible preprocessing pipelines that can be constructed from four fundamental image transforms: histogram equalization~(E), intensity normalization~(N), non-local means denoising~(D), and color space conversion~(CS). By evaluating every permutation at every pipeline length from one through four---64 non-baseline configurations plus the unprocessed baseline---for a total of 65 evaluated pipelines, the study generates a complete map of the pipeline performance space. This combinatorial design enables a direct decomposition of preprocessing performance into the contributions of transform selection and transform ordering, quantified through variance analysis and permutation-based statistical tests.

The principal findings of this study are fourfold. First, under multi-seed replication ($n = 5$ seeds per configuration) a Holm-corrected permutation test confirms a statistically significant length-degradation effect: the correlation between pipeline length and mean accuracy gain is $r = -0.44$ ($p_{\mathrm{Holm}} = 0.0013$), and the proportion of pipelines with a mean $\alpha > 0$ collapses from 25\% at $k=1$ to 0\% at lengths two through four against the 98.09\% baseline. Second, variance decomposition reveals a crossover from selection-dominated variance at $k=2$ ($\eta^2 = 0.76$, not significant after multiple-testing correction) to ordering-dominated variance at $k=3$, where ordering accounts for 58\% of the total variance in $\alpha$ ($\eta^2_{\text{selection}} = 0.42$, $F(3,20) = 4.83$, $p_{\mathrm{Holm}} = 0.022$); at $k=4$, ordering is the sole source of variance by construction and produces a 3.78-pp swing across the 24 permutations of the full transform set. Third, positional analysis groups the four transforms along a three-level spectrum of ordering sensitivity: equalization is strongly position-sensitive with a statistically significant preference for the first position (Cohen's $d = 1.22$, $p_{\mathrm{Holm}} = 0.0005$); normalization and color space conversion are moderately position-sensitive with opposite preferences (late and early, respectively); and denoising is approximately position-neutral. Fourth, confusion-matrix decomposition at the default decision threshold indicates that 78\% of tested pipelines exhibit bidirectional degradation (both false-positive and false-negative counts increase) rather than a decision-threshold shift (22\%); the balance moves toward threshold-shift dominance (48\%) only at a higher confidence threshold ($\tau = 0.7$), suggesting that calibration-based recovery of lost accuracy is threshold-regime-dependent rather than universally applicable.

These findings are synthesized into a practical pipeline design template, $\text{E} \to \{\text{D},\text{CS}\} \to \text{N}$, that places distribution-dependent operations at the pipeline boundary and permits relatively free arrangement of interior operations whose positional sensitivity is weaker. The remainder of this paper is organized as follows: Section~\ref{sec:related} reviews related work on preprocessing evaluation in medical imaging. Section~\ref{sec:methods} describes the experimental methodology, including the combinatorial design and statistical framework. Section~\ref{sec:results} presents the results. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses the theoretical and practical implications. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.

% II. RELATED WORK
% ===================================================================
\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

The role of image preprocessing in medical image classification has been investigated across several clinical domains, though the majority of prior work evaluates individual transforms rather than their combinatorial interactions~\cite{Koresh2024}. This section reviews the existing literature on preprocessing evaluation, identifies the methodological gap addressed by the present study, and situates the contribution within the broader context of pipeline design for computer-aided diagnosis.

Rodrigues~et~al.~\cite{RODRIGUES2020103542} evaluated six preprocessing strategies for HEp-2 cell classification in immunofluorescence images, including the original unprocessed image, mean subtraction, contrast stretching (with and without mean subtraction), and histogram equalization (with and without mean subtraction). Their study found, counterintuitively, that the highest accuracy was achieved using unprocessed images for most CNN architectures, with Inception-V3 reaching 96.69\% on the original images. Only ResNet-50 benefited from contrast stretching. This result highlights a key tension in the preprocessing literature: transforms that are beneficial in isolation may offer no advantage, or may even degrade performance, when applied to architectures capable of learning equivalent representations from raw data. However, the study evaluated each strategy independently and did not examine multi-stage pipelines or the effect of transform ordering.

In the biometric authentication domain, Tarawneh~et~al.~\cite{Tarawneh2024} investigated the effect of mean and median filtering on CNN-based dorsal hand vein recognition. Without preprocessing, their model achieved 70\% accuracy on the training set; with mean filtering, accuracy rose to 99\%. While this study demonstrates that appropriate preprocessing can yield dramatic improvements, it again evaluated single transforms in isolation, without considering how the combination or ordering of multiple transforms might affect the results.

For dermatoscopic image analysis specifically, deep learning has achieved dermatologist-level performance on skin cancer classification~\cite{Esteva2017}, motivating extensive work on preprocessing pipelines for this modality. Arora~et~al.~\cite{ARORA2021102358} proposed a modified U-Net architecture incorporating group normalization and attention gates for skin lesion segmentation, achieving segmentation accuracies of 95--97\% across four public databases. Dash~et~al.~\cite{DASH2020106240} developed a cascaded deep CNN framework for psoriasis severity assessment that included intensity normalization and artifact removal as preprocessing steps. Nugroho~et~al.~\cite{10810559} examined the effect of preprocessing, specifically hair removal, contrast enhancement, and noise reduction, on skin lesion segmentation accuracy, demonstrating that appropriate preprocessing improved segmentation performance. In each case, the preprocessing pipeline was designed and applied as a fixed sequence without systematic exploration of alternative orderings.

Av\c{s}ar~\cite{9548351} evaluated the effects of image preprocessing on CNN performance for pneumonia detection, testing preprocessing techniques including Wiener filtering and histogram equalization. The study demonstrated that preprocessing selection significantly affected classification accuracy, but, like the other works reviewed here, did not address the ordering of multi-stage pipelines. Vincent and Roopa Jayasingh~\cite{9780692} surveyed preprocessing techniques for psoriasis detection, cataloguing segmentation, feature extraction, and classification approaches without examining how the sequencing of these operations affects downstream accuracy.

A consistent methodological gap emerges from this review. Existing studies treat preprocessing transforms as independent interventions whose effects can be evaluated in isolation. No prior work, to our knowledge, has conducted an exhaustive combinatorial evaluation of all possible orderings of a given set of transforms, nor has any study decomposed the variance in classification performance into the separate contributions of transform selection and transform ordering. The present study fills this gap by evaluating all 65 pipeline configurations constructible from four transforms, enabling a direct comparison between the predictive power of knowing \emph{which} transforms are in a pipeline versus knowing \emph{how} they are ordered.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methods}

% --- A. Dataset and Architecture ---
\subsection{Dataset and Classification Architecture}
\label{subsec:dataset}

The experimental dataset was constructed by combining dermatoscopic images of skin lesions from the HAM10000 collection~\cite{DBLP:journals/corr/abs-1803-10417, Codella2019} with images of healthy skin from a subset of the DERM12345 dataset~\cite{yilmaz2024derm12345largemultisourcedermatoscopic}, forming a class-balanced binary classification task comprising 20{,}000 images (10{,}000 diseased and 10{,}000 healthy). Images were resized to a uniform $224 \times 224$ spatial resolution prior to model ingestion; no data augmentation was applied, so as to isolate the effect of preprocessing from stochastic training-time perturbations.

The classification architecture consisted of a four-block convolutional neural network (CNN). Each block comprised a convolutional layer, batch normalization~\cite{IoffeSzegedy2015}, ReLU activation~\cite{NairHinton2010}, and max pooling. Filter counts doubled at each block, 32, 64, 128, and 256, followed by fully connected layers reducing dimensionality to a single output node with sigmoid activation for binary classification. This four-block topology was chosen as a minimal yet representative purely convolutional baseline: it is deep enough to learn the hierarchical feature abstractions required for dermatoscopic classification yet free of the residual connections or self-attention mechanisms whose invariance properties are themselves known to modulate sensitivity to input distortions. Holding the architecture fixed isolates preprocessing-induced variation from architectural confounds and makes the ordering-sensitivity question, how much does transform arrangement affect a representative convolutional feature extractor, answerable; the corresponding question for attention-based architectures is deferred to Section~\ref{subsec:limitations}.

The dataset was partitioned into training (80\%, $n = 16{,}000$), validation (10\%, $n = 2{,}000$), and test (10\%, $n = 2{,}000$) subsets. Training used the Adam optimizer~\cite{KingmaBa2015} with a learning rate of $10^{-4}$ and binary cross-entropy (BCE) as the loss function, which is the proper scoring rule for the sigmoid output used here~\cite{Good1952}; an earlier version of this work employed mean squared error, and the switch to BCE removes the gradient-shrinkage pathology that MSE exhibits when applied to a saturated sigmoid. To separate preprocessing-induced variation from stochastic variation in initialization, shuffling, and data partitioning, every pipeline was trained independently under five random seeds ($s \in \{42, 123, 256, 512, 1024\}$), with the dataset split regenerated per seed so that training, validation, and test partitions differ across seeds but remain identical across all 65 pipelines within a given seed. Within each seed, the PyTorch, NumPy, and CUDA random-number generators were seeded identically prior to dataset splitting and weight initialization, so that dataset partitioning and the initial convolutional-layer weights are seed-deterministic and shared across all 65 pipelines evaluated under that seed. One residual source of intra-seed nondeterminism remains: the DataLoader worker processes used for training-batch shuffling are not explicitly re-seeded in the present implementation, so minibatch ordering during training can differ between otherwise-identical pipeline runs within a seed. The bootstrap 95\% confidence intervals reported throughout absorb this nondeterminism alongside initialization and split-level variability; a fully-deterministic DataLoader configuration would tighten the intervals but would not alter the mean-level comparisons that drive the principal findings. Under these design constants, a paired five-seed difference-of-means test is powered ($1 - \beta = 0.80$, two-sided $\alpha_{\text{level}} = 0.05$) to detect per-pipeline accuracy-gain contrasts of approximately $\pm 1.0$\,pp and arm-level contrasts (e.g., equalization-first versus not-first, pooling 16 versus 48 pipelines) of approximately $\pm 0.35$\,pp; contrasts below these thresholds should therefore be interpreted as under-resolved by the present experimental design rather than as null results. All numerical results reported in Sections~\ref{sec:results} onward are seed-aggregated means with bootstrap 95\% confidence intervals (10{,}000 resamples) unless otherwise stated. All experiments were executed on a system equipped with an AMD Ryzen 9 9950X3D processor, 96\,GB of DDR5 RAM, and an NVIDIA GeForce RTX 4080 SUPER GPU running CUDA~12.8.

% --- B. Preprocessing Transforms ---
\subsection{Preprocessing Transforms}

Four fundamental image transforms were selected for evaluation, spanning the principal categories of preprocessing commonly employed in medical imaging pipelines~\cite{Brinker2019, RODRIGUES2020103542, Tarawneh2024, computers8030062}: intensity standardization, contrast enhancement, noise suppression, and color representation.

\textbf{Intensity Normalization (N)} applies an affine transformation $x' = \sigma_t \cdot \frac{x - \mu_{\text{in}}}{\sigma_{\text{in}}} + \mu_t$ to rescale pixel values toward a target mean $\mu_t$ and standard deviation $\sigma_t$, where $\mu_{\text{in}}$ and $\sigma_{\text{in}}$ are the empirical statistics of the input image. This standardization reduces inter-sample variability in lighting and contrast, allowing the network optimizer to converge more efficiently~\cite{10.5555/1076432}.

\textbf{Histogram Equalization (E)} redistributes pixel intensities to approximate a uniform cumulative distribution function~\cite{10.5555/1076432, 9025640, 10810559, Reza2004}. By expanding the dynamic range, equalization enhances subtle textural and chromatic contrasts that may be diagnostically relevant in skin lesion assessment.

\textbf{Non-Local Means Denoising (D)} suppresses acquisition noise by computing weighted averages over similar image patches~\cite{Buades2005}, using a template window and a search window~\cite{MAFI2019236, elad2023imagedenoisingdeeplearning}. Unlike local filters, non-local means preserves repetitive patterns and edge structures characteristic of dermatological lesions.

\textbf{Color Space Conversion (CS)} transforms the image from RGB to HSV representation, separating luminance from chrominance information~\cite{WANG2018157, ballester2022influencecolorspacesdeep}. Because RGB-to-HSV conversion is bijective on the unit cube, all three transformed channels (H, S, V) are passed to the network rather than a projection, so no intensity information is discarded by the transform itself; the HSV representation merely re-parameterizes pixel coordinates so that the network can learn color-based diagnostic features that are entangled in the RGB basis.

Preprocessing parameters were determined through a grid search evaluating 95 configurations in total (36 normalization parameter pairs, 55 denoising template/search-window pairs, and 4 color-space variants). Each configuration was trained and evaluated under the seed-42 data partition and the best single-transform parameters, reported in Table~\ref{tab:params}, were then held fixed across all 65 pipelines and all five replication seeds. Because the grid search consumed the same data partition used for main-experiment evaluation at seed 42, the reported accuracies are mildly optimistic for that seed; the bootstrap confidence intervals reported throughout are therefore seed-level, not grid-search-level, uncertainty estimates. A fully held-out parameter-tuning split is a natural extension but was not pursued here given the fixed preprocessing-study scope.

% --- C. Combinatorial Experimental Design ---
\subsection{Combinatorial Experimental Design}

The central methodological contribution of this study is the exhaustive evaluation of all possible preprocessing pipelines constructible from the four selected transforms. At each pipeline length $k \in \{1, 2, 3, 4\}$, every ordered permutation of $k$ distinct transforms drawn from the set $\mathcal{T} = \{\text{E}, \text{N}, \text{D}, \text{CS}\}$ with $|\mathcal{T}| = 4$ was evaluated independently. The total number of non-baseline pipelines is therefore
\begin{equation}
f(x=4) = \sum_{k=1}^{x} \frac{x!}{(x - k)!} = 4 + 12 + 24 + 24 = 64,
\label{eq:combinatorics}
\end{equation}
which, together with the unprocessed baseline ($k=0$), yields 65 evaluated configurations; each of the 65 was trained from scratch under each of the five seeds listed in Section~\ref{subsec:dataset}, for a total of 325 training runs. Each trained model was then evaluated at both the base decision threshold $\tau = 0.5$ and the high-confidence threshold $\tau = 0.7$; the two regimes share the same underlying training runs and differ only in the decision rule applied at test time.

This design is distinguished from prior work in two respects. First, it evaluates all orderings of each transform subset, not merely a single conventional ordering. For instance, the three-element subset $\{\text{E}, \text{D}, \text{N}\}$ is evaluated in all six orderings: E$\to$D$\to$N, E$\to$N$\to$D, D$\to$E$\to$N, D$\to$N$\to$E, N$\to$D$\to$E, and N$\to$E$\to$D. Second, by spanning all pipeline lengths, the design enables direct statistical comparison of performance as a function of pipeline complexity.

% --- D. Performance Metrics ---
\subsection{Performance Metrics}

Three metrics were drafted to quantify preprocessing impact. The accuracy gain $\alpha$ measures the absolute change in test-set accuracy:
\begin{equation}
\alpha = \varepsilon_x - \varepsilon_0
\label{eq:alpha}
\end{equation}
where $\varepsilon_x$ is the accuracy of the preprocessed model and $\varepsilon_0$ is the seed-matched baseline accuracy without preprocessing ($98.09\% \pm 0.19\%$ across five seeds). Positive $\alpha$ indicates improvement; negative $\alpha$ indicates degradation. Because the baseline is computed per seed rather than against a single fixed value, $\alpha$ is paired with and within-seed, which increases statistical power relative to an unpaired comparison against a fixed reference.

The time ratio $\gamma$ quantifies computational overhead:
\begin{equation}
\gamma = \frac{\rho_x}{\rho_0}
\label{eq:gamma}
\end{equation}
where $\rho_x$ and $\rho_0$ are the training times with and without preprocessing, respectively.

The cost-adjusted accuracy gain $\alpha_w$ integrates both performance and computational cost:
\begin{equation}
\alpha_w =
\begin{cases}
\dfrac{\alpha}{\gamma}, & \text{if } \alpha \geq 0 \\[0.4em]
\alpha \cdot \gamma, & \text{if } \alpha < 0.
\end{cases}
\label{eq:alpha_w}
\end{equation}
The asymmetry is intentional: when a pipeline improves accuracy ($\alpha \geq 0$) its merit is reduced by any computational overhead ($\gamma > 1$), which divides the benefit and rewards pipelines that achieve the same gain at lower cost; when a pipeline degrades accuracy ($\alpha < 0$) the penalty is \emph{amplified} rather than attenuated by computational overhead, since $\alpha \cdot \gamma$ is more negative than $\alpha$ whenever $\gamma > 1$. A continuous metric such as $\dfrac{\alpha}{\gamma}$ applied on both branches would instead have softened the penalty on slow, inaccurate pipelines. Readers seeking a sign-symmetric alternative may consider $\dfrac{\alpha}{\gamma}$ uniformly; the ranking of pipelines in Table~\ref{tab:results} does not change qualitatively under that substitution. Quantitatively, the Spearman rank correlation between the piecewise $\alpha_w$ of~\eqref{eq:alpha_w} and uniform $\dfrac{\alpha}{\gamma}$ across the 64 non-baseline pipelines is $\rho = 0.89$ (permutation $p < 10^{-22}$), and eight of the ten pipelines most highly ranked under $\alpha_w$ are also among the ten most highly ranked under uniform $\dfrac{\alpha}{\gamma}$, so downstream design conclusions derived from $\alpha_w$ are robust to the choice of asymmetric versus sign-symmetric weighting.

% --- E. Statistical Analysis Methods ---
\subsection{Statistical Analysis Methods}
\label{subsec:stats}

Five statistical techniques are employed to characterize the structure of the pipeline performance landscape. The Holm--Bonferroni correction described at the end of this subsection is applied across a pre-specified family of five primary tests: (i) the Pearson correlation between pipeline length and seed-averaged $\alpha$ (Section~\ref{subsec:length_degradation}); (ii) the equalization-first-placement contrast (Section~\ref{subsec:positional}); (iii) the bookend E-first/N-last contrast (Section~\ref{subsec:positional}); (iv) the one-way ANOVA on $\alpha$ grouped by unordered transform set at pipeline $k=2$ (Section~\ref{subsec:variance}); and (v) the analogous one-way ANOVA at pipeline $k=3$. The per-pipeline false-discovery-rate analysis described below constitutes a \emph{separate} family of 64 per-pipeline tests and is not included in the Holm family.

\textbf{Bootstrap confidence intervals} are computed for every per-pipeline mean $\alpha$ by resampling the five seed-level measurements with replacement 10{,}000 times and taking the 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles of the resulting distribution of means. These intervals propagate seed-to-seed variability into all downstream point estimates reported throughout Section~\ref{sec:results}. The per-pipeline intervals are \emph{marginal} rather than simultaneous; family-wise control across the 64 pipelines is delivered separately through the Benjamini--Hochberg step-up procedure described at the end of this subsection.

\textbf{Variance decomposition} via one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) quantifies the relative contribution of transform selection (between-set variance) versus transform ordering (within-set variance) at each pipeline length. The effect size $\eta^2$ measures the proportion of total variance explained by transform-set membership, and its uncertainty is quantified by a nonparametric bootstrap over the five per-seed $\alpha$ measurements: each of 10{,}000 iterations draws five seed indices with replacement, recomputes the seed-averaged group means, and evaluates the resulting $\eta^2$; the 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles of the distribution define the reported 95\% confidence interval. A complementary per-seed pooled $\eta^2$ that treats each pipeline's five seed-level $\alpha$ values as separate observations is reported alongside the seed-averaged estimate, providing a diagnostic for the extent to which the seed-averaging step inflates the between-group variance relative to the full within-group dispersion. Residual normality is verified with the Shapiro--Wilk test at $\alpha_{\text{level}} = 0.05$, and violations are flagged alongside the reported F-statistic.

\textbf{Permutation tests} with 100{,}000 iterations assess the statistical significance of observed effects without distributional assumptions~\cite{Good2005}. Two-sample permutation tests are paired with Cohen's $d$ as the effect-size measure; correlation permutation tests use the observed Pearson $r$ as test statistic. In the pooled arm-level tests (equalization-first contrast, bookend contrast, length--$\alpha$ correlation) the exchangeable unit under the null is the seed-averaged per-pipeline $\alpha$, and permutations reassign pipelines between arms without breaking the within-pipeline seed pairing that gave rise to the aggregate $\alpha$. A complementary per-seed test conditional on pipeline-length stratum is reported in Section~\ref{subsec:positional} to address the concern that pipeline-length confounding could inflate the bookend contrast.

A per-pipeline false-discovery-rate (FDR) analysis complements the pooled arm-level tests. For each of the 64 non-baseline pipelines, a two-sided bootstrap $p$-value for the null $H_0: \alpha = 0$ is computed by centering the five seed-level $\alpha$ measurements at zero and resampling $10{,}000$ times with Phipson--Smyth $+1$ smoothing to avoid zero-valued $p$-values. The resulting family of 64 $p$-values is then corrected using the Benjamini--Hochberg step-up procedure~\cite{BenjaminiHochberg1995} at $q = 0.05$, a less conservative alternative to Holm--Bonferroni that is appropriate for the large per-pipeline family where family-wise error control would eliminate most individual pipelines as a matter of design. The BH-corrected per-pipeline verdicts are reported in Section~\ref{subsec:results_overview} alongside the bootstrap confidence intervals.

\textbf{Positional analysis} computes the mean $\alpha$ achieved by each transform as a function of its ordinal position across all pipelines in which it appears, revealing position-dependent performance gradients.

\textbf{Confusion-matrix decomposition} tracks the change in false positives ($\Delta\text{FP}$) and false negatives ($\Delta\text{FN}$) relative to the seed-matched baseline for each pipeline. A pipeline is classified as a \emph{threshold-shift} case if the two deltas carry opposite sign (one error type increases, the other decreases) and as a \emph{bidirectional degradation} case if both increase.

\textbf{Multiple-testing correction} is applied across the family of five primary permutation and ANOVA tests reported below using the Holm--Bonferroni method~\cite{Holm1979}. All $p$-values attached to named hypothesis tests in Section~\ref{sec:results} are Holm-corrected unless explicitly labeled ``raw'' for transparency; uncorrected values are reported parenthetically when they differ substantially.

\textbf{Gradient-weighted Class Activation Mapping} (Grad-CAM)~\cite{8237336} was applied to the final convolutional layer of each trained model (seed 42 only) to visualize the spatial regions most influential to the classifier's prediction. For each of the 65 pipeline configurations, Grad-CAM saliency maps were generated for six test images sampled at three confidence strata per class: a high-confidence correct prediction, a decision-boundary case ($p(\text{diseased} \mid x) \in [0.45, 0.55]$), and a near-misclassification case. Both positive-class (diseased) and negative-class (healthy) activation overlays were produced, yielding 12 visualizations per pipeline per confidence regime. Because Grad-CAM analyses of single images are qualitative rather than inferential, the maps presented in Section~\ref{subsec:gradcam} are used only as an illustrative adjunct to the quantitative findings and are not treated as evidence for any statistical claim.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

% --- A. Pipeline Performance Overview ---
\subsection{Pipeline Performance Overview}
\label{subsec:results_overview}

Table~\ref{tab:results} reports the per-pipeline five-seed mean accuracy gain ($\alpha$) and mean cost-adjusted accuracy gain ($\alpha_w$) for all 65 configurations, together with 10{,}000-resample bootstrap 95\% confidence intervals for $\alpha$. The per-seed baseline accuracies average $\varepsilon_0 = 98.09\% \pm 0.19\%$, a level already close to the ceiling for this binary task; the range of achievable preprocessing-induced accuracy changes is correspondingly narrow and is mostly directed toward degradation.

Across the 64 non-baseline pipelines, only one configuration attains a positive mean $\alpha$: equalization alone ($\alpha = +0.22$\,pp, $\alpha_w = +0.002$, CI$_{95\%} = [-0.07, +0.50]$\,pp), and its confidence interval narrowly straddles zero. All 60 multi-transform pipelines produce negative mean $\alpha$, and the remaining three single-transform pipelines (denoising, normalization, color-space conversion) are statistically indistinguishable from baseline (all CIs overlap zero). The per-pipeline Benjamini--Hochberg FDR analysis described in Section~\ref{subsec:stats} confirms this asymmetry at the individual-pipeline level: at $q = 0.05$, 49 of 64 pipelines (76.6\%) are flagged as having a significantly \emph{negative} mean $\alpha$ and zero pipelines as having a significantly positive mean $\alpha$, with the remaining 15 pipelines indistinguishable from baseline under FDR control. In the \emph{base} confidence regime ($\tau = 0.5$), the preprocessing-design task is therefore better framed as harm minimization than as accuracy maximization: the research question of practical relevance is which configurations do the least damage, not which produce positive net gain.

Harm ranking follows a clear structure. Among length-two pipelines, E$\to$D ($\alpha = -0.17$\,pp), E$\to$N ($-0.28$\,pp), and D$\to$E ($-0.53$\,pp) carry the smallest penalties, all with CIs that include zero. Among length-three pipelines, E$\to$N$\to$D ($-0.12$\,pp), E$\to$D$\to$N ($-0.25$\,pp), and E$\to$CS$\to$N ($-0.43$\,pp) are least detrimental, each placing equalization first. Among length-four pipelines, no configuration achieves a CI that covers zero; the least damaging are D$\to$E$\to$CS$\to$N ($-0.51$\,pp) and E$\to$CS$\to$D$\to$N ($-0.61$\,pp), again with equalization positioned early and normalization terminally. The most detrimental pipeline at $k=4$ is N$\to$D$\to$CS$\to$E at $\alpha = -4.29$\,pp, followed by CS$\to$N$\to$E$\to$D ($-3.83$\,pp) and CS$\to$D$\to$N$\to$E ($-3.38$\,pp); all three place normalization first and equalization last, inverting the empirically favorable template identified in Section~\ref{subsec:positional}.

% --- B. Length Degradation ---
\subsection{Statistical Validation of the Length-Degradation Effect}
\label{subsec:length_degradation}

To determine whether the observed decline in mean $\alpha$ with increasing pipeline length reflects a genuine length-dependent mechanism or merely a combinatorial artifact --- longer pipelines explore more of the configuration space and are therefore more likely to include poor orderings by chance --- a permutation test was conducted across the 64 non-baseline pipelines. The Pearson correlation between pipeline length (1 to 4 transforms) and seed-averaged $\alpha$ yielded $r = -0.44$, indicating a moderate negative association. Under the null hypothesis of no length effect, $\alpha$ values were randomly reassigned to length categories (preserving group sizes of 4, 12, 24, and 24) across 100{,}000 permutations. Only 0.032\% of permuted datasets produced a correlation as extreme as the observed value (raw $p = 0.00032$; Holm-corrected $p_{\text{Holm}} = 0.00128$ across the five primary tests), establishing that the negative association between pipeline length and accuracy gain is statistically significant and not attributable to the combinatorial structure of the experimental design.

The proportion of pipelines achieving positive mean $\alpha$ decreases monotonically with length: 25\% at $k=1$ (1 of 4, corresponding to equalization alone), and 0\% at each of lengths 2, 3, and 4. In the base confidence regime, multi-transform preprocessing is therefore not merely risky but statistically incapable, within the resolution of a 5-seed evaluation, of producing a net accuracy gain at all. The length-degradation effect is driven not by a few catastrophic outliers at $k=4$ but by a consistent downward shift of the entire distribution: mean $\alpha$ across non-baseline pipelines is $-0.27$\,pp at $k=1$, $-1.19$\,pp at $k=2$, $-1.55$\,pp at $k=3$, and $-2.02$\,pp at $k=4$.

% --- C. Variance Decomposition ---
\subsection{Variance Decomposition: Ordering Versus Selection}
\label{subsec:variance}

To quantify the relative contribution of transform selection versus transform ordering, the total variance in seed-averaged $\alpha$ among multi-stage pipelines was decomposed into a between-set component (attributable to which transforms are included) and a within-set component (attributable to how they are arranged) at each pipeline length. Pipelines sharing the same unordered set of transforms were grouped together, and a one-way ANOVA was computed with transform set as the grouping factor; residuals were checked for approximate normality via the Shapiro--Wilk test before ANOVA results were interpreted.

At pipeline $k=2$, where each of the six possible transform pairs admits only two orderings, transform selection accounts for the majority of variance at the seed-averaged level ($\eta^2 = 0.761$, $F(5,6) = 3.81$, raw $p = 0.067$; Shapiro--Wilk $p = 0.74$). The $F$-test has only six residual degrees of freedom and is correspondingly underpowered: the 10{,}000-resample nonparametric bootstrap over the five per-seed $\alpha$ measurements yields a 95\% confidence interval of $[0.56, 0.89]$ for the seed-averaged $\eta^2$, which excludes zero but spans more than a third of the unit interval. The raw $p$-value does not cross the conventional 5\% threshold and, under Holm--Bonferroni correction across the five primary tests, the length-2 ANOVA remains non-significant. A complementary per-seed pooled $\eta^2$, which treats each pipeline's five seed-level $\alpha$ values as separate observations and so absorbs within-pipeline seed variance into the residual, falls to $\eta^2 = 0.22$. The seed-averaged point estimate therefore overstates the selection effect by conflating between-pipeline variance with between-set variance, and the length-2 selection-dominated claim should be read as a directional point estimate rather than as a statistically confirmed result.

This relationship inverts at pipeline $k=3$, where each of the four possible transform triplets admits six orderings. The within-set (ordering) component now accounts for 58\% of total variance, while the between-set (selection) component accounts for 42\% ($\eta^2 = 0.420$, $F(3,20) = 4.83$, raw $p = 0.011$, $p_{\text{Holm}} = 0.022$; Shapiro--Wilk $p = 0.99$). The selection effect is therefore still statistically detectable at $k=3$ under multiple-testing correction, but it accounts for less than half of the observable variation in pipeline performance.

At pipeline $k=4$, all 24 pipelines share the same transform set $\{\text{E}, \text{N}, \text{D}, \text{CS}\}$, so ordering is the sole source of variance by construction. The within-set standard deviation of $\alpha$ at this length is 1.07\,pp and the range is 3.78\,pp (from $-0.51$\,pp for D$\to$E$\to$CS$\to$N to $-4.29$\,pp for N$\to$D$\to$CS$\to$E), approximately three times the 1.33\,pp range observed across the four single-transform pipelines and large relative to the 0.19\,pp standard deviation of the baseline itself.

The progression from selection-dominated variance at $k=2$ to mixed selection--ordering contribution at $k=3$ to pure ordering variance at $k=4$ admits a combinatorial interpretation: with two transforms there are only two orderings per set, limiting the scope for ordering-induced divergence; with three transforms there are six orderings, and with four there are 24, providing increasingly many opportunities for detrimental arrangements to emerge.

% --- D. Positional Preferences ---
\subsection{Transform Positional Preferences and the Bookend Structure}
\label{subsec:positional}

To investigate whether specific transforms exhibit statistically significant positional preferences, the seed-averaged mean $\alpha$ achieved by each transform was computed as a function of its ordinal position (first through fourth) across all pipelines in which it appears. This positional analysis reveals a complementary structure between equalization and normalization.

Equalization exhibits a monotonically deteriorating mean $\alpha$ as it is moved from position~1 to position~4 (from $-0.74$\,pp to $-3.12$\,pp), corresponding to a linear gradient of approximately $+0.78$\,pp per position toward the front of the pipeline. Normalization displays the mirror-image pattern (from $-2.25$\,pp at position~1 to $-0.91$\,pp at position~4), a gradient of approximately $+0.45$\,pp per position toward the end of the pipeline. Because position $k$ is by definition accessible only to pipelines of length $\geq k$, and because the length-degradation effect of Section~\ref{subsec:length_degradation} shifts mean $\alpha$ downward at longer lengths, a length-stratified control is required before these pooled gradients can be interpreted as genuine positional effects rather than as a reflection of the length composition of each position arm. The stratified analysis, summarized in Table~\ref{tab:positional}, isolates the within-length first-versus-last contrast for each transform and shows that equalization's first-position advantage grows monotonically with length ($+0.40$\,pp at $k=2$, $+1.20$\,pp at $k=3$, $+2.13$\,pp at $k=4$), that normalization's last-position advantage grows symmetrically ($-0.75$\,pp, $-1.22$\,pp, $-1.91$\,pp across the same lengths, where negative deltas indicate that the first-position mean is worse than the last-position mean), and that denoising and color-space conversion remain near-flat within every length stratum. The equalization and normalization positional effects therefore survive length stratification; the pooled per-position gradients reported above are dominated by genuine position dependence rather than by a confound with pipeline length.

To assess whether equalization's first-position advantage reflects a genuine positional effect rather than a selection effect, two complementary permutation tests were run. The pooled test partitions the 64 non-baseline pipelines into equalization-first ($n = 16$) and all-others ($n = 48$, including 33 pipelines that contain equalization at a non-initial position and 15 that do not contain equalization at all); it yields a $+1.10$\,pp advantage with Cohen's $d = 1.22$ (raw $p = 0.0001$, $p_{\text{Holm}} = 0.0005$). The pooled control, however, blends pipelines that omit equalization with those that merely position it later, conflating first-position placement with the decision to include equalization at all. A within-inclusion test that restricts the control arm to the 33 pipelines containing equalization at a non-initial position yields a cleaner separation: the advantage rises to $+1.22$\,pp (Cohen's $d = 1.39$, raw $p = 0.0001$, $p_{\text{Holm}} \leq 0.0005$), confirming that the first-position effect is driven by placement rather than inclusion. Equalization is the only transform among the four for which first-position placement achieves significance after multiple-testing correction under either the pooled or within-inclusion specification.

Denoising exhibits a near-flat positional profile (gradient $\approx -0.17$\,pp/position); the corresponding within-length contrasts (Table~\ref{tab:positional}) are $-0.33$, $-0.39$, and $+0.13$\,pp at lengths 2, 3, and 4 and do not approach significance. Color-space conversion shows a weak negative gradient ($\approx -0.06$\,pp/position) in the pooled analysis, and its within-length contrasts ($+0.67$, $+0.41$, $-0.35$\,pp) change sign with length, indicating no consistent positional preference at the resolution of this experimental design.

The complementary positional structure is further confirmed by a bookend analysis examining the joint effect of first and last transforms. Pipelines adopting the equalization-first, normalization-last configuration ($n = 5$ across lengths 2--4) achieve a mean $\alpha$ of $-0.52$\,pp, substantially less detrimental than the inverse configuration --- normalization-first, equalization-last --- which produces a mean $\alpha$ of $-2.53$\,pp. The $+2.00$\,pp contrast between these two bookend configurations is large (Cohen's $d = 2.38$) and statistically significant after correction (raw $p = 0.008$, $p_{\text{Holm}} = 0.025$), confirming that the equalization--normalization ordering is not interchangeable. Within the base confidence regime, the E--N bookend ordering is best interpreted as the configuration that minimizes harm rather than one that recovers positive accuracy gain.

To verify that the bookend contrast reflects a genuine ordering effect rather than a confound with pipeline length --- the E-first/N-last and N-first/E-last arms are not uniformly distributed across lengths 2, 3, and 4 --- the test was repeated within each length stratum using per-seed $\alpha$ values as the resampling unit. Cohen's $d$ for the E--N bookend contrast is 1.83 ($n = 5$ versus $5$, permutation $p = 0.048$) at $k=2$, 1.86 ($n = 10$ versus $10$, $p < 10^{-4}$) at $k=3$, and 1.52 ($n = 10$ versus $10$, $p < 10^{-5}$) at $k=4$. The effect size is therefore large and statistically significant within every length stratum individually, ruling out pipeline-length confounding as the driver of the pooled bookend result.

% --- E. Error Decomposition ---
\subsection{Error Decomposition: Bidirectional Degradation at Base Confidence}
\label{subsec:error}

To characterize the mechanism through which preprocessing degrades classification accuracy, the change in false positives ($\Delta\text{FP}$) and false negatives ($\Delta\text{FN}$) relative to the per-seed baseline model was computed for each pipeline, then averaged across seeds. Contrary to the single-seed preliminary analysis reported in the submitted version of this manuscript, the multi-seed data do not exhibit a strong $\Delta\text{FP}/\Delta\text{FN}$ anti-correlation: the Pearson correlation between the two error-shift variables across the 64 non-baseline pipelines is $r = +0.04$, essentially zero and in the opposite sign to what a pure threshold-shift mechanism predicts.

Categorizing each pipeline by the sign pattern of its error shifts yields the following distribution. Only 14 of 64 pipelines (21.9\%) exhibit a threshold-shift pattern in which false positives and false negatives move in strictly opposite directions: 10 (15.6\%) shift the operating point toward the diseased classification ($\Delta\text{FP} > 0$, $\Delta\text{FN} < 0$), and 4 (6.2\%) shift it toward the healthy classification. The remaining 50 pipelines (78.1\%) exhibit bidirectional degradation, in which both error types increase relative to baseline; no pipeline in the base regime produces a bidirectional improvement in which both error types decrease.

This distribution of mechanisms inverts the pattern reported in the preliminary single-seed results and is the central methodological consequence of the five-seed replication: the dominant effect of preprocessing at the default $\tau = 0.5$ threshold is not a recoverable rotation of the operating point along the ROC curve but an irreversible loss of class-discriminative structure in the learned feature space. The implications for post-hoc calibration as a recovery strategy are correspondingly weaker than initially suggested; a threshold-shift interpretation is sustained for only a minority of configurations.

A separate analysis at the higher decision threshold $\tau = 0.7$ (the ``high-confidence'' regime reported in the supplementary analysis file) yields a distinctly different mechanism mixture, with threshold-shift cases accounting for approximately half of the pipelines. The full high-confidence analysis is beyond the scope of the main narrative but is retained in the released analysis artifact; it suggests that the threshold-shift signature emerges primarily when the classifier is operated at a conservative threshold rather than at the default $\tau = 0.5$, and that calibration-based recovery strategies should be evaluated in a regime-conditional manner.

% V. DISCUSSION

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

% --- A. Ordering Invariance ---
\subsection{CNN Feature Representations and Ordering Invariance}

The variance decomposition reveals a finding that, while consistent with the mathematical non-commutativity of nonlinear image transformations, is far from trivially predictable: the four-block CNN architecture employed here is not ordering-invariant. A sufficiently expressive feature extractor could, in principle, learn representations that abstract away the differences between alternative orderings of the same transform set, rendering ordering irrelevant to classification accuracy. The empirical result --- that ordering accounts for 58\% of the variance in $\alpha$ at pipeline $k=3$ and is the sole source of variance at $k=4$, with a 3.78\,pp range in mean $\alpha$ across the 24 length-4 pipelines --- demonstrates that the convolutional architecture used here lacks this invariance property. The feature-space distortions introduced by different orderings are large enough to propagate through the entire network to the classification layer.

The progression from selection-dominated variance at $k=2$ ($\eta^2_{\text{selection}} = 0.76$) to mixed selection--ordering contribution at $k=3$ ($\eta^2_{\text{selection}} = 0.42$) to pure ordering variance at $k=4$ admits a combinatorial interpretation: with two transforms there are only two orderings per set; with three there are six; with four, 24. The combinatorial argument alone does not explain why mean $\alpha$ declines with length, only why variance grows, but it identifies ordering proliferation as the mechanism through which longer pipelines explore larger regions of the performance space, with the overwhelming majority of those regions corresponding to degradation relative to the unprocessed baseline.

The practical implication of the ordering effect is that the conventional approach to preprocessing pipeline design --- selecting a set of beneficial transforms and applying them in a conventionally motivated order --- is insufficient for pipelines of $k=3$ or greater. At these lengths, the choice of arrangement is a substantial determinant of \emph{how much} a pipeline degrades accuracy, and evaluating a single ordering of a promising transform set provides little information about the performance of alternative orderings. It must be emphasized, however, that within the scope of this evaluation the choice of arrangement modulates the magnitude of harm rather than the sign of the accuracy change: no multi-transform ordering of any subset of $\{\text{E}, \text{D}, \text{N}, \text{CS}\}$ produced a positive mean $\alpha$ at the default $\tau = 0.5$ threshold.

% --- B. Mechanistic Interpretation ---
\subsection{Distribution-Dependent Versus Distribution-Agnostic Transforms}

The statistically validated positional preferences of equalization and normalization admit a coherent mechanistic interpretation grounded in the mathematical properties of each transform. Histogram equalization is a nonlinear, data-dependent remapping that redistributes pixel intensities to approximate a uniform cumulative distribution~\cite{Pizer1987}. Because this redistribution is computed from the empirical histogram of the input image, its output, and hence the features it enhances, depends critically on the distributional properties of its input. When equalization operates on the raw image (first position), it has access to the full dynamic range and the native intensity distribution, maximizing its capacity to reveal subtle textural contrasts in dermatological lesions. When preceding transforms have already altered the intensity distribution, particularly normalization, which compresses the distribution toward a fixed mean and standard deviation, the histogram from which equalization computes its mapping is informationally impoverished, and the resulting enhancement is correspondingly degraded.

Normalization, by contrast, is a linear affine transformation ($x' = \frac{x - \mu}{\sigma}$) that is agnostic to the distributional shape of its input; its sole function is to shift and scale pixel values to match the statistical expectations of the downstream convolutional network. This operation is therefore most effective when applied immediately before network ingestion, regardless of what transforms precede it. The mirror-image positional gradients observed empirically, equalization improving toward the front, normalization improving toward the back, are thus direct consequences of these distinct mathematical sensitivities: a distribution-dependent nonlinear operation requires pristine input distributions, while a distribution-agnostic linear standardizer benefits from being the final transform before the network.

More broadly, the positional analysis suggests a three-level spectrum of preprocessing operations: \emph{strongly position-sensitive} transforms whose effectiveness depends on the distributional properties of their input (exemplified by equalization, with a gradient of $\approx +0.78$\,pp/position toward the front and a bookend Cohen's $d = 2.38$ against its inverse ordering); \emph{moderately position-sensitive} transforms (exemplified by normalization at $\approx +0.45$\,pp/position toward the end); and \emph{position-neutral} transforms (denoising and color-space conversion, with gradients below $0.2$\,pp/position in absolute value). For practitioners designing preprocessing pipelines, this spectrum implies a heuristic: distribution-dependent nonlinear operations should be placed as early as possible to preserve their access to native image statistics, while linear standardizers should be deferred to the final pipeline stage. Applied to the four-transform case, this heuristic yields the $\text{E} \to \{\text{D}, \text{CS}\} \to \text{N}$ template, where denoising and color-space conversion occupy interior positions largely interchangeably. Within the scope of this study, the template is to be understood as the arrangement that minimizes harm at the default decision threshold rather than one that yields net accuracy improvement.

% --- C. Error Mechanism ---
\subsection{Feature Degradation and the Limits of Calibration-Based Recovery}

The multi-seed error-decomposition analysis reverses the tentative conclusion suggested by preliminary single-seed data: at the default decision threshold, preprocessing-induced accuracy changes predominantly reflect genuine feature-space degradation rather than threshold shifts. Of the 64 non-baseline pipelines evaluated in the base regime, 78\% produce bidirectional increases in both false-positive and false-negative counts relative to the seed-matched baseline, while only 22\% exhibit opposite-sign error shifts consistent with a pure operating-point displacement; the aggregate $\Delta\text{FP}/\Delta\text{FN}$ correlation is approximately zero ($r = +0.04$). Bidirectional degradation is the signature of transformation sequences that reduce the discriminative structure of the representation ingested by the classifier, not merely the balance point between its two error types.

This distribution of mechanisms has direct consequences for the viability of calibration-based recovery strategies~\cite{Liang2022, Guo2017}. Post-hoc threshold adjustment can only redistribute errors along the existing ROC curve; it cannot recover accuracy that was lost because the pipeline altered the curve itself. Since the aggregate behavior at $\tau = 0.5$ is dominated by feature degradation rather than threshold displacement, the scope for calibration to offset suboptimal preprocessing is inherently limited in this regime. In the conservative-threshold regime ($\tau = 0.7$), however, the mechanism mixture shifts: approximately half of the pipelines exhibit threshold-shift signatures, suggesting that the viability of post-hoc calibration is a function of operating point as well as pipeline identity.

For clinical systems, the practical implication is that preprocessing pipelines must be evaluated against the operating threshold at which the system will actually be deployed. The identification of the mechanism mixture under which a pipeline degrades accuracy --- bidirectional versus threshold-shift --- determines whether recalibration is a plausible remediation and whether a given ordering can be safely recovered without retraining. The single-threshold analyses that dominate the preprocessing literature cannot distinguish these cases and risk mistaking recoverable operating-point displacement for irreversible feature destruction, or vice versa.

% --- D. Practical Guidelines ---
\subsection{Practical Pipeline Design Guidelines}

The results presented in this study converge on a set of evidence-based guidelines for preprocessing pipeline design in medical image classification.

First, the statistically significant length-degradation effect ($r = -0.44$, $p_{\text{Holm}} = 0.00128$), combined with the observation that no multi-transform configuration achieved positive mean $\alpha$ at the default threshold, establishes that simpler pipelines are not merely computationally cheaper but substantially less likely to harm classification accuracy. Practitioners seeking to improve classification on near-ceiling binary tasks similar to the one evaluated here should first verify that preprocessing is actually necessary at all: the best achievable configuration in this study was the unprocessed baseline itself, with equalization alone representing the only single transform whose 95\% CI for $\alpha$ did not lie strictly below zero.

Second, when domain or deployment constraints require multi-stage preprocessing, the positional analysis provides a concrete design template. Distribution-dependent, nonlinear transforms (exemplified by equalization, $d = 1.22$ for first-position placement) should occupy the earliest pipeline position to operate on unprocessed image statistics. Interior transforms (denoising, color-space conversion) may be placed largely interchangeably between the boundary operations, as their positional gradients are small in absolute value. Linear standardizers (normalization) should be deferred to the final position immediately before network ingestion, with the bookend configuration $\text{E} \to \cdots \to \text{N}$ producing a $+2.00$\,pp mean reduction in harm ($d = 2.38$) relative to its inverse.

Third, the mechanism analysis implies that preprocessing should be evaluated jointly with the operating threshold. The dominance of bidirectional degradation at $\tau = 0.5$ and its partial substitution by threshold-shift signatures at $\tau = 0.7$ indicate that preprocessing pipelines cannot be ranked independently of the decision threshold: a pipeline whose errors are recoverable by recalibration at one threshold may impose irreversible feature-space losses at another.

Fourth, the ordering effect at pipeline lengths of three or more implies that evaluating a single ordering of a promising transform set provides little information about the performance of alternative orderings of the same set. Any systematic evaluation of multi-stage preprocessing must either enumerate all orderings or employ the positional template proposed here to constrain the search to a tractable subset of likely-least-harmful configurations.

% --- E. Limitations ---
\subsection{Limitations and Future Directions}
\label{subsec:limitations}

Several limitations constrain the generality of these findings. The evaluation is restricted along four axes that each independently bound external validity: a single four-block CNN architecture, a single binary classification task (healthy versus diseased) constructed from a single dataset pairing (HAM10000 combined with the DERM12345 corpus, $n = 20{,}000$ balanced images), a single input resolution ($224 \times 224$), and a single per-transform hyperparameter configuration obtained by grid search on one of the five seeds (Table~\ref{tab:params}). Each of these choices is common in the preprocessing-evaluation literature, but the combination means that the template derived here---equalization first, normalization last---is established within a narrow slice of the design space rather than across it. Modern architectures incorporating attention mechanisms, residual connections~\cite{He2016}, or vision transformers~\cite{Dosovitskiy2021} may exhibit different sensitivity profiles to preprocessing ordering; multi-class lesion subtyping, cross-modality inputs (dermatoscopic combined with clinical photography), and non-dermatological medical imaging may exhibit different transform--position interactions; and alternative resolution or parameter settings may reweight the relative contributions of selection and ordering. The conclusions drawn here accordingly constitute an existence proof that ordering effects can dominate selection effects in a practically relevant regime, not a claim that the specific template will transfer unchanged across architectures, tasks, or datasets.

A second limitation, closely coupled to the first, is that the binary healthy-versus-diseased task is already near-ceiling for this dataset ($\varepsilon_0 = 98.09\%$), which arithmetically bounds the maximum detectable positive $\alpha$ to at most $+1.91$\,pp. The observation that no multi-transform pipeline produced net accuracy gain is accordingly not dispositive evidence that preprocessing is universally harmful; it is consistent both with a genuine no-gain regime and with a ceiling-bounded regime in which any latent positive effect is smaller than the detectable margin. Pipelines that appear uniformly harmful here may behave differently on harder tasks such as multi-class lesion subtyping where baseline accuracy is lower, where the available headroom for preprocessing gains exceeds the seed-to-seed noise floor, and where transform selection may therefore recover a larger share of the variance than the $42\%$ observed at $k=3$. The ordering-dominance finding, by contrast, is less exposed to the ceiling: ordering effects manifest as \emph{differences} between pipelines at matched length, and the within-length variance decomposition is computed after subtracting the ceiling-bounded mean. The five-seed replication provides sufficient power to detect moderate positional effects (bookend $d = 2.38$, E-first $d = 1.22$) but is underpowered to resolve small per-pipeline contrasts, and the bootstrap confidence intervals at $k=2$ and $k=4$ in particular remain wide for several configurations.

Because preprocessing hyperparameters (Table~\ref{tab:params}) were selected on the seed-42 data partition and then held fixed across all five replication seeds, a residual concern is that seed-42 grid-search leakage could inflate the reported effect sizes for the pipelines whose parameters happened to be well-matched to that partition. To test this concern directly, a leave-one-seed-out (LOSO) sensitivity analysis was conducted in which each of the five primary tests (length--$\alpha$ correlation, equalization-first contrast, bookend contrast, length-2 and length-3 ANOVA) was recomputed on each of the five four-seed subsets in turn, with Holm--Bonferroni correction re-applied within each recomputation. The equalization-first contrast remains significant in all five LOSO runs with Cohen's $d \in [1.12, 1.29]$, and the length--$\alpha$ correlation remains significant in all five with $r \in [-0.48, -0.41]$. The bookend contrast is significant in all five LOSO runs with $d \in [1.89, 3.21]$ across a full $d = 2.38$ at the five-seed level; the smallest LOSO effect size ($d = 1.89$, obtained when seed~1024 is excluded) and the largest ($d = 3.21$, obtained when seed~123 is excluded) bracket the pooled estimate from both sides rather than from a single direction, which is inconsistent with the hypothesis that any single tuned seed drives the pooled bookend effect. The LOSO run that excludes the grid-search seed itself (seed~42) yields $d = 2.32$, within $0.06$ of the full-data estimate --- closer to the pooled value than any other single-seed exclusion --- which is the signature expected if seed-42 leakage has negligible effect on the bookend contrast rather than a leakage-driven inflation.

The four length-one pipelines, the only stratum in which any configuration attains a nonnegative mean $\alpha$, are the single-transform configurations most directly exposed to leakage concerns, because their parameters were grid-searched on seed~42 individually. LOSO analysis of these four pipelines is correspondingly sharper than at longer lengths: equalization retains a positive LOSO mean across all five four-seed subsets ($\bar\alpha \in [+0.10, +0.33]$\,pp); denoising remains near zero ($\bar\alpha \in [-0.17, +0.19]$\,pp); normalization straddles zero with approximately symmetric perturbation ($\bar\alpha \in [-0.33, +0.21]$\,pp); and color-space conversion remains strictly negative in every LOSO run ($\bar\alpha \in [-1.24, -0.97]$\,pp). The central qualitative claim --- that equalization alone is the only single-transform configuration whose 95\% CI for $\alpha$ does not lie strictly below zero --- is robust to seed-42 exclusion, ruling out the interpretation that the equalization-alone result is an artifact of the grid-search partition.

The length-3 ANOVA is the least robust of the five primary tests under seed-level perturbation. Its full-data result ($F(3,20) = 4.83$, raw $p = 0.011$, $p_{\text{Holm}} = 0.022$) gives a Holm-corrected significance, but the LOSO analysis yields Holm-corrected $p$-values of $\{0.009, 0.050, 0.118, 0.121, 0.363\}$ across the five runs, of which only two clearly cross $p_{\text{Holm}} < 0.05$ and one more sits exactly on the threshold. Under raw $p$-values, four of five LOSO runs cross $p_{\text{raw}} < 0.05$, so the length-3 selection effect is directionally consistent across seeds but only one seed exclusion (of seed~256) is sufficient to extinguish its Holm-corrected significance. The length-3 ANOVA conclusion should accordingly be interpreted as suggestive rather than definitive; the narrative of a selection-to-ordering variance crossover relies on a signal that is detectable at full-data resolution but sensitive to single-seed perturbation.

A systematic decision-threshold sweep, which reviewers of an earlier version of this manuscript identified as the natural continuation of the base/high-confidence comparison reported in Section~\ref{subsec:error}, would require persisting per-image predicted probabilities across all 325 training runs in each confidence regime. The present released artifacts record per-pipeline accuracy and confusion-matrix summaries rather than full per-image probability tensors, so the sweep cannot be executed retroactively without a full re-training of the experimental matrix. Extending the released analysis to include per-image probability tensors at each seed, and reporting the accuracy--threshold curve $\varepsilon_x(\tau)$ for every pipeline, is accordingly deferred to a follow-up evaluation.

The four transforms evaluated here, while representative of the principal categories of image preprocessing, do not exhaust the space of operations commonly applied in medical imaging. Learned preprocessing layers, adaptive filtering techniques, and domain-specific artifact removal (e.g., hair removal in dermoscopy) may exhibit different positional sensitivities and interact differently within multi-stage pipelines.

Additionally, the experimental design evaluates each pipeline as a fixed preprocessing step applied identically to all images. In clinical practice, images arrive with heterogeneous quality characteristics, and an adaptive preprocessing strategy that selects and orders transforms conditionally on input image properties, analogous to learned augmentation policies~\cite{Shorten2019, Cubuk2019, Cubuk2020}, may achieve better overall performance than any fixed pipeline.

Future work should investigate how preprocessing ordering interacts with modern architectures, particularly whether the self-attention mechanism in vision transformers provides greater robustness to ordering variation, potentially absorbing distortions that the purely convolutional architecture studied here cannot. The development of preprocessing-aware calibration procedures represents a promising direction for the subset of pipelines that do exhibit threshold-shift signatures, particularly at higher operating thresholds. The Grad-CAM illustration in Section~\ref{subsec:gradcam} demonstrates that ordering alters the spatial distribution of learned features; a systematic same-image comparison across all 65 pipeline configurations, controlling for confidence stratum and class label, would enable quantitative characterization of how activation topology varies with ordering and pipeline length, yielding deeper mechanistic understanding of the ordering-sensitivity phenomenon. Finally, extending the multi-seed evaluation to a larger seed count ($n \geq 10$) and to multi-class classification tasks would sharpen the per-pipeline confidence intervals and test whether the no-net-gain result observed here persists once the ceiling effect of binary classification is relaxed.

% VI. CONCLUSION
% ===================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This study provides a multi-seed, statistically corrected characterization of how transform ordering and transform selection jointly determine the performance of fixed preprocessing pipelines applied to a near-ceiling binary skin-classification task. Through exhaustive evaluation of all 65 pipeline configurations constructible from four fundamental image transforms, replicated across five random seeds and analyzed with bootstrap confidence intervals, Holm-corrected permutation tests, and variance decomposition, the work establishes four findings with direct practical relevance.

First, the statistically significant length-degradation effect ($r = -0.44$, $p_{\text{Holm}} = 0.00128$) establishes that each additional preprocessing step carries a measurable risk of harm. In the $\tau = 0.5$ regime evaluated here, only equalization alone produced a 95\% CI for $\alpha$ that did not lie strictly below zero; no multi-transform configuration achieved a positive mean $\alpha$. This no-gain observation must, however, be read in conjunction with the $98.09\%$ baseline: the task's arithmetic ceiling bounds the detectable positive-$\alpha$ regime to $+1.91$\,pp, and the result accordingly establishes the absence of multi-transform gain in a near-ceiling regime rather than across the full difficulty spectrum of medical image classification. Second, the variance decomposition identifies ordering as a substantial source of performance variation at lengths three and four (58\% and 100\% of variance respectively), confirming that the four-block CNN architecture employed here is not ordering-invariant. Third, positional analysis identifies a concrete harm-minimizing template: equalization should occupy the first pipeline position (E-first effect $d = 1.22$ against the pooled not-first control and $d = 1.39$ against the within-inclusion control, $p_{\text{Holm}} = 0.0005$) and normalization the last, with the bookend contrast between $\text{E} \to \cdots \to \text{N}$ and its inverse yielding a $+2.00$\,pp difference in mean $\alpha$ ($d = 2.38$, $p_{\text{Holm}} = 0.025$). Fourth, the confusion-matrix analysis indicates that approximately 78\% of preprocessing-induced accuracy changes at $\tau = 0.5$ reflect bidirectional feature-space degradation rather than recoverable threshold displacement, tempering the applicability of calibration-based remediation strategies at this operating point, though a partial shift toward threshold-shift signatures is observed at the higher threshold $\tau = 0.7$.

Within the scope of this single CNN, single binary task, single dataset pairing, single input resolution, and single per-transform parameter configuration, these findings motivate a reframing of preprocessing pipeline design as harm minimization rather than accuracy maximization. The template $\text{E} \to \{\text{D}, \text{CS}\} \to \text{N}$ derived from the positional analysis is best interpreted not as a prescription for improvement over the unprocessed baseline but as the least-detrimental multi-transform arrangement consistent with the distributional requirements of each operation, and only within the ceiling-bounded, near-$98\%$ baseline regime evaluated here. Whether this template generalizes to harder tasks (multi-class lesion subtyping, non-dermatological medical imaging), to non-ceiling baseline regimes, to modern attention-based or transformer architectures, to alternative resolutions and per-transform parameter settings, and to adaptive preprocessing policies that select transforms conditionally on input image properties or per-sample valuation criteria~\cite{Yoon2020}, are the principal open questions for follow-up work. The present evidence establishes that ordering effects \emph{can} dominate selection effects in a practically relevant slice of the preprocessing design space; it does not license the inverse claim that they universally do.
