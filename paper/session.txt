  H1: Non-Commutative Feature-Space Operator Hypothesis

  Statement: Image preprocessing transforms act as non-commutative operators on
  the learned feature space, where the order of application produces
  path-dependent representations that differentially interact with the CNN's
  convolutional filters.

  Mechanistic explanation: Each transform modifies the pixel distribution that
  the subsequent transform receives. Equalization redistributes histogram mass
  uniformly; normalization then scales this uniform distribution to a target
  mean/std. Reversing the order (N→E) first compresses dynamic range, then
  equalization attempts to re-spread an already-truncated distribution,
  amplifying quantization noise. The non-commutativity arises because these
  operations are not linear — equalization is a monotonic but nonlinear
  remapping, and its Jacobian depends on the input distribution.

  Key supporting evidence from the data:
  - Same transform set {E, N} yields α = +0.0058 in order E→N but α = -0.0133 in
   order N→E (spread = 0.019)
  - The full 4-transform set {CS, D, E, N} has a spread of 0.058 across its 24
  orderings, from +0.005 to -0.053
  - All 12 bigrams show that N→{anything} is consistently the worst first
  element (all 11 instances of N→CS and all 11 of N→E produce negative mean α)

  Testable predictions:
  1. Grad-CAM activation maps for the best-ordering (E→N) and worst-ordering
  (N→E) of the same transform set should show qualitatively different spatial
  attention patterns — the former attending to lesion boundaries, the latter to
  background texture
  2. The feature distribution (at the penultimate FC layer) after E→N should
  have higher inter-class separability (measured by Fisher's discriminant ratio)
   than after N→E
  3. The magnitude of the order effect should correlate with the nonlinearity of
   the individual transforms: pairs involving equalization (highly nonlinear)
  should show larger order sensitivity than pairs involving only normalization
  and color space conversion (approximately linear)

  Experimental design: Apply each of the 12 possible ordered bigrams, extract
  intermediate feature maps after each convolutional block, and compute the
  Centered Kernel Alignment (CKA) similarity between the resulting feature
  representations. Non-commutativity would manifest as low CKA between permuted
  orderings of the same transform set.

  H2: Equalization-as-Gatekeeper Hypothesis

  Statement: Histogram equalization uniquely functions as a "gatekeeper"
  transform whose position in the pipeline determines whether subsequent
  transforms encounter a feature-extractable or feature-degraded input, and this
   gatekeeping role intensifies as the classification task becomes more
  sensitive to preprocessing.

  Mechanistic explanation: Equalization establishes a uniform histogram that
  maximizes information entropy. When applied first, all downstream transforms
  operate on a maximally informative distribution. When equalization is
  deferred, preceding transforms (especially normalization) compress the
  distribution's information content, and late equalization amplifies the
  resulting noise rather than the signal. The effect intensifies in the high_con
   regime because the classification boundary is narrower, making the model more
   sensitive to the quality of its input distribution.

  Key supporting evidence:
  - In high_con, equalization at position 1 is the only transform with positive
  mean α (+0.0044); at position 2 it drops to -0.0700, at position 3 to -0.1344
  - In base, equalization at position 1 has mean α = -0.0052 (near-neutral), but
   at position 3 drops to -0.0171
  - The 3x amplification of the positional degradation from base→high_con (3.3x
  at position 2, 7.9x at position 3) suggests a multiplicative interaction with
  task difficulty

  Testable predictions:
  1. The Shannon entropy of the image histogram after equalization-first should
  be significantly higher than after any other transform-first
  2. The downstream transform's Jacobian norm (a measure of transform
  sensitivity) should be lower when equalization precedes it, indicating more
  stable transformation behavior
  3. On a third experimental regime with even higher baseline accuracy (>99%),
  equalization-first should be the only pipeline with non-negative α

  H3: Specificity-Erosion Hypothesis

  Statement: Detrimental preprocessing pipelines systematically erode
  specificity (true negative rate) while preserving or increasing sensitivity,
  because aggressive transforms selectively destroy the subtle texture features
  necessary for identifying healthy skin while amplifying the more salient
  features of diseased skin.

  Mechanistic explanation: Skin lesions typically have high-contrast, spatially
  localized features (irregular borders, color variation), while healthy skin
  identification depends on low-contrast, distributed texture uniformity.
  Aggressive preprocessing — particularly denoising followed by normalization —
  smooths the fine-grained texture patterns that distinguish healthy skin from
  pathological skin, while the high-contrast lesion features survive the
  transformation. This creates an asymmetric error: the model over-predicts
  disease.

  Key supporting evidence:
  - The worst pipeline (E→D→N→CS, α = -0.053) has sensitivity increase of +0.018
   but specificity decrease of -0.125
  - Of the 5 worst pipelines, 3 show increased sensitivity with decreased
  specificity
  - Overall, 17 of 64 pipelines improve sensitivity while degrading specificity
  (quadrant 4), vs. only 5 that improve both
  - Baseline confusion matrix is roughly balanced (FP=21, FN=14), but worst
  pipelines skew heavily (FP=96, FN=3 for the worst)

  Testable predictions:
  1. Grad-CAM maps for false positives produced by the worst pipelines should
  show activation over background/healthy-skin regions that were not present in
  the baseline model's attention
  2. If the dataset is enriched with more subtle/borderline healthy skin
  samples, the specificity erosion effect should amplify
  3. Replacing Non-Local Means denoising with an edge-preserving filter (e.g.,
  bilateral filter) should reduce the specificity erosion for pipelines
  containing denoising, because edge-preserving filters maintain the texture
  gradients that distinguish healthy skin

  H4: Regime-Dependent Optimality Reversal Hypothesis

  Statement: The optimal preprocessing pipeline for a given classification task
  is not a fixed property of the transforms but a function of the model's
  operating regime (baseline accuracy), and beyond a critical baseline accuracy
  threshold, the optimal and worst pipelines undergo partial or complete rank
  inversion.

  Mechanistic explanation: At lower baselines, the model under-extracts
  features, and preprocessing that enhances relevant features (contrast, color
  separation) provides genuine signal amplification. At higher baselines, the
  model's learned filters already capture the relevant signal, and preprocessing
   acts as a perturbation to an already-optimized feature space. The rank
  inversion occurs because transforms that provide the strongest signal
  amplification (e.g., CS→E→N) also produce the strongest perturbation when
  applied to already-optimal features.

  Key supporting evidence:
  - 42% (27/64) of pipelines flip sign between base and high_con
  - The best pipeline in base (CS→E→N, α = +0.0157) becomes α = -0.1582 in
  high_con — a complete reversal from best to worst quartile
  - Conversely, N→E→CS, negative in base (α = -0.0265), becomes positive in
  high_con (α = +0.0091)
  - The mean alpha shifts from -0.0112 (base) to -0.0581 (high_con), but the
  variance also increases dramatically (more extreme positive and negative
  outliers)

  Testable predictions:
  1. If experiments are conducted at 3+ baseline accuracy levels (e.g., 85%,
  90%, 95%, 98%), the rank correlation (Kendall's τ) between pipeline rankings
  should decrease monotonically as the baseline difference increases
  2. There exists a crossover baseline accuracy level where the previously-best
  pipeline (CS→E→N) transitions from positive to negative α — this critical
  point can be estimated by interpolation
  3. Pipelines with the smallest absolute α at one regime should show the most
  stable ranking across regimes (they are "regime-invariant"), while pipelines
  with the largest absolute α are the most regime-sensitive

  H5: Computational Cost Ceiling Hypothesis

  Statement: Beyond two sequential transforms, additional preprocessing steps
  impose computational costs that grow superlinearly while accuracy gains
  exhibit diminishing (or negative) returns, creating a Pareto frontier where
  single-transform and two-transform pipelines dominate the cost-adjusted
  performance space.

  Mechanistic explanation: Each additional transform both extends wall-clock
  time and introduces a compounding opportunity for feature degradation. The
  probability that a random k-length pipeline is beneficial decreases
  combinatorially with k, because each additional transform must be compatible
  with the feature space produced by all preceding transforms. Meanwhile, the
  training-time overhead of preprocessing accumulates approximately linearly
  (each transform adds I/O and computation), creating an asymmetric cost-benefit
   curve.

  Key supporting evidence:
  - Pipeline length vs. performance (base): Length-1 mean α = +0.0031, Length-2

  - The best αw values cluster at Length-1 and Length-2: Equalization alone (αw

  0.0082)
  - The mean gamma increases: Length-1 = 0.794, Length-2 = 0.866, Length-3 =
  0.978, Length-4 = 1.131

  1. A Pareto analysis of (αw, pipeline length) should show that no Length-4
  pipeline lies on the Pareto frontier
  2. The probability of a random k-length pipeline being beneficial should decay

  3. If a fifth transform type is introduced (e.g., data augmentation,

  Critical Assessment of the Five Hypotheses

  Overall Verdict

  Two are solid (H3, H5), one is promising but needs tighter specification (H2),

  ---
  Cross-Cutting Methodological Concerns

  Before examining each hypothesis, four structural problems affect the entire
  set:

  1. Post-hoc hypothesis generation from a single dataset. All five hypotheses
  were derived from the same 130 experimental runs. I looked at the data, found
  patterns, and then framed those patterns as "hypotheses." This is textbook
  HARKing (Hypothesizing After Results are Known). The patterns are real
  observations, but calling them "hypotheses" implies they were formulated
  independently of the evidence used to support them. Properly, these are
  empirical observations that generate testable predictions — the predictions
  are the actual hypotheses, and they require new data (a different dataset,
  architecture, or transform set) to be tested.

  2. Pseudoreplication inflates apparent sample sizes. The 64 pipelines are not
  independent. They share transforms, training data, architecture, and random
  seed. A pipeline containing {E, N, D} is not independent of one containing {E,
   N} — they share two-thirds of their transforms. When I report "16 pipelines
  with Equalization first," these 16 are highly correlated. The effective
  degrees of freedom are far lower than the raw counts suggest, and none of my
  pattern summaries include confidence intervals or statistical tests.

  3. No null model for comparison. When I report that "42% of pipelines flip
  sign between regimes," I never asked: what flip rate would you expect under 
  the null hypothesis that preprocessing effects are pure noise? If α values are
   normally distributed around zero in both regimes (with different variances),
  the expected sign-concordance depends on the signal-to-noise ratio and can
  easily be ~50%. A 42% discordance rate might be less than chance expectation —
   which would actually support cross-regime consistency, the opposite of what
  H4 claims.

  4. Single architecture, single dataset, binary task. Every pattern might be
  specific to this 4-block CNN on HAM10000 with binary classification. The
  hypotheses are stated as general principles about preprocessing, but the
  evidence base cannot support that generality.

  ---
  H1: Non-Commutative Feature-Space Operator Hypothesis

  Rating: Weak — near-tautological

  The core claim — that nonlinear operations applied in different orders produce
   different results — is a mathematical identity, not a scientific hypothesis.
  Composing two nonlinear functions f∘g ≠ g∘f is true by definition for almost
  all nonlinear f, g. Stating this as a hypothesis is like hypothesizing that
  addition is commutative.

  The interesting scientific question is buried inside: which specific 
  feature-space distortions cause the CNN to fail, and can we predict them from 
  properties of the transforms? But the hypothesis doesn't answer this. The CKA
  prediction ("low CKA between permuted orderings") is guaranteed to be true for
   any nonlinear transforms and tells us nothing about whether the model will
  perform well or poorly.

  The third prediction — "magnitude of order effect should correlate with
  nonlinearity of individual transforms" — is the only substantive one, but
  "nonlinearity" is undefined. By what measure? Jacobian norm? Entropy change?
  Lipschitz constant? Without specifying this, the prediction is unfalsifiable.

  What to salvage: Drop the framing as a hypothesis. Instead, treat the
  order-sensitivity data as motivation for H2, which makes a more specific and
  falsifiable claim about which ordering matters and why.

  ---
  H2: Equalization-as-Gatekeeper Hypothesis

  Rating: Promising but overfit to two data points

  The positional gradient for equalization is the strongest pattern in the data:
   E at position 1 has mean α = +0.0044 (high_con), dropping to -0.0700 at
  position 2 and -0.1344 at position 3. This is a genuine, striking signal.

  However:

  - The claim that this effect "intensifies as the classification task becomes
  more sensitive to preprocessing" is based on comparing exactly two conditions
  (base and high_con). You cannot establish a monotonic trend from two points.
  The prediction about a third regime (>99% accuracy) is pure extrapolation.
  - The mechanism (equalization maximizes information entropy, enabling
  downstream transforms to work on a maximally informative distribution) is
  plausible but unverified. Equalization is also the most nonlinear of the four
  transforms, so its position sensitivity might simply be a special case of
  "more nonlinear transforms are more position-sensitive" — which would make H2
  a corollary of H1, not an independent hypothesis.
  - The effective sample sizes are small: "E at position 1" groups 16 pipelines,
   but these share the E-first constraint and differ only in what follows. The
  16 are not independent measurements of "E-first effectiveness."

  The entropy prediction is the strongest part. Measuring Shannon entropy after
  E-first vs. after other-first is simple, requires no new training, and would
  directly test the proposed mechanism.

  H3: Specificity-Erosion Hypothesis

  Rating: Strongest of the five, but the mechanism is speculative

  The confusion matrix evidence is compelling: the worst pipeline (E→D→N→CS, α =
   -0.053) shifts from FP=21/FN=14 (baseline) to FP=96/FN=3 — a dramatic
  specificity collapse with simultaneous sensitivity increase. This is not
  noise; it's a systematic shift in the model's decision behavior.

  Concerns:

  - A simpler explanation exists: the model's decision threshold might be
  shifting rather than its feature representations. If preprocessing
  systematically increases the model's output confidence toward "diseased,"
  you'd see the same sens+/spec- pattern without any texture-specific mechanism.
   A sigmoid output shift would explain this parsimoniously. Before invoking the
   "texture destruction" mechanism, one should check whether the model's
  confidence distribution (not just its binary predictions) shifts
  systematically.
  - The pattern holds for only 3 of the 5 worst pipelines. The 4th worst shows
  spec- but also sens-, and the 2nd worst shows sens- but spec+. A 60% hit rate
  isn't robust for the hypothesis's core claim.
  - The Grad-CAM prediction is good — spatial attention shifting toward
  background regions would genuinely distinguish the threshold-shift explanation
   from the texture-destruction explanation. This is the right test.

  What would strengthen it: Before claiming texture destruction, plot the
  distribution of sigmoid outputs (pre-threshold) for the baseline model vs. the
   worst pipeline. If the entire distribution shifts rightward (toward
  "diseased"), it's a threshold effect. If the distribution becomes bimodal or
  changes shape, it's a feature-space change.

  H4: Regime-Dependent Optimality Reversal Hypothesis

  Rating: Potentially built on a data error — needs verification

  This is the most problematic hypothesis because of an unresolved data
  provenance issue.

  The critical problem: The JSON data shows identical baseline accuracy (97.10%)
   for both "base" and "high_con" conditions. Yet the paper's table reports ε₀ =
   88.5% and ε₀ = 98.3%. If both JSON conditions share the same baseline, then
  the two "regimes" are not distinguished by baseline accuracy — they're
  distinguished by something else (different hyperparameters, different
  preprocessing parameter grids, different training durations). The entire
  hypothesis about accuracy-dependent reversal might be based on a
  misunderstanding of what "base" vs. "high_con" actually represents.

  I failed to investigate this discrepancy before formulating the hypothesis.
  That's a significant methodological error in the hypothesis generation process
   itself.

  Even assuming the regimes do differ in baseline:

  - Two regimes is the minimum to observe a difference and the maximum to
  misinterpret it. You cannot establish rank inversion as a general principle
  from n=2. The Kendall's τ prediction requires at least 4-5 regime levels.
  - The 42% discordance rate (27/64) needs comparison against a null model. If
  pipeline alphas are drawn from a distribution with mean slightly below zero in
   both regimes (which matches the data: mean α = -0.011 and -0.058), the
  expected sign-discordance depends on the per-pipeline variance. Without this
  null comparison, 42% might be unremarkable.

  Before this hypothesis can be taken seriously, the "base" vs. "high_con"
  distinction in the data must be clarified. If they share the same baseline
  accuracy, the hypothesis needs complete reformulation.

  ---
  H5: Computational Cost Ceiling Hypothesis

  Rating: Solid observation, but the quantitative model is overfitted

  The qualitative claim — that longer pipelines have worse cost-adjusted
  performance — is well-supported by a monotonic trend across all four pipeline
  lengths. The Pareto frontier prediction is concrete and testable.

  Concerns:

  - The exponential decay model P(α > 0) ∝ e^{-λk} is fitted to four data
  points: {(1, 0.75), (2, 0.42), (3, 0.21), (4, 0.08)}. Four points can fit
  almost any two-parameter model. This isn't a tested prediction; it's
  curve-fitting.
  - There's a combinatorial confound that the hypothesis ignores entirely. With
  4 available transforms, a length-1 pipeline has 4 possible orderings, length-2
   has 12, length-3 has 24, and length-4 has 24. Longer pipelines explore more
  of the combinatorial space, mechanically increasing the probability of
  encountering both extreme-good and extreme-bad outcomes. The mean degradation
  with length could simply reflect that there are more ways to arrange 
  transforms badly than well, a combinatorial argument that requires no
  reference to "compounding feature degradation."

  - To test this: if you randomly shuffled all 64 alpha values and assigned them
   to pipeline lengths proportionally, would you still observe a declining mean?
   If yes, the effect is combinatorial, not causal.
  - The small counts at length-1 (n=4) make the 75% estimate extremely fragile.
  One different result changes it to 50% or 100%.

  What would strengthen it: Conduct a permutation test — randomly reassign
  pipeline lengths to alpha values 10,000 times and check whether the observed
  monotonic decline in mean α is in the tail of the null distribution. This
  would distinguish a real length effect from a combinatorial artifact.

  detection as hypothesis formulation. The honest framing is: "We observed
  patterns P1–P5 in the data. These generate predictions Q1–Q5, which can be
  tested on new data." Calling P1–P5 "hypotheses supported by evidence" is
  circular — the evidence is the hypothesis source. This distinction
  (observation vs. prediction) is the difference between exploratory and
  confirmatory analysis, and conflating them inflates the apparent strength of
  the "hypotheses."

  They are different preprocessing hyperparameter sets, not different baseline 
  accuracy levels. The baseline model is identical in both conditions — same
  97.10% accuracy, same confusion matrix, same Grad-CAM images. The paper's ε₀ =
   88.5% and ε₀ = 98.3% come from a different experimental run than the JSON

  ★ Insight ─────────────────────────────────────
  Why this matters: H4 claimed that "the optimal pipeline reverses when the
  model operates in a different accuracy regime." What's actually happening is
  far more mundane: different preprocessing hyperparameters produce different
  outcomes. Of course they do — that's the entire point of parameter
  optimization. The "42% sign discordance" that seemed dramatic is actually less
   than what you'd expect under statistical independence (51.7%), and the
  permutation test confirms the discordance is unremarkable (p = 0.98). The
  Kendall's τ = 0.15 shows weak-but-positive rank correlation — there is some
  consistency between parameter sets, just not much. This is a lesson in
  verifying data provenance before theorizing.
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────

  What can be salvaged: The weak cross-parameter-set rank correlation (τ = 0.15)
   does carry a legitimate, if less dramatic, message: pipeline ordering 
  recommendations are sensitive to preprocessing hyperparameters. A pipeline
  optimized with one parameter set (e.g., normalization mean=0.4, std=0.2) does
  not reliably transfer to another parameter set (mean=0.15, std=0.75). This is
  worth noting in the paper but doesn't need a standalone hypothesis — it's a
  methodological caveat about the parameter grid search.

  The paper's Discussion section already discusses the ceiling effect using the
  ε₀ = 88.5% vs 98.3% data from the original experimental runs. That analysis
  remains valid for its own data — the JSON just can't corroborate it because
  it's a different experiment.

  H3 is Partially Overturned — Threshold Shift is Real, but Bidirectional

  The systematic analysis of all 64 pipelines' confusion matrices reveals:

  FP-change and FN-change are anti-correlated: r = -0.545.

  This is the signature of a decision threshold shift, not a selective feature
  destruction. When a pipeline increases false positives, it simultaneously
  decreases false negatives (and vice versa). The model isn't losing the ability
   to recognize healthy skin — it's shifting where it draws the line between
  "healthy" and "diseased."

  The error-shift quadrant breakdown:

  ┌─────────┬───────┬───────┬────────┬───────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Pattern │ Count │   %   │ Mean α │            Interpretation             │
  ├─────────┼───────┼───────┼────────┼───────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ FP-/FN+ │ 26    │ 40.6% │ -0.013 │ Threshold → healthy (sensitivity      │
  │         │       │       │        │ erosion)                              │
  ├─────────┼───────┼───────┼────────┼───────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ FP+/FN- │ 20    │ 31.2% │ -0.011 │ Threshold → disease (specificity      │
  │         │       │       │        │ erosion)                              │
  ├─────────┼───────┼───────┼────────┼───────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ FP+/FN+ │ 11    │ 17.2% │ -0.018 │ General degradation                   │
  ├─────────┼───────┼───────┼────────┼───────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ FP-/FN- │ 7     │ 10.9% │ +0.009 │ General improvement                   │
  └─────────┴───────┴───────┴────────┴───────────────────────────────────────┘

  H3 claimed that detrimental pipelines "systematically erode specificity while 
  preserving sensitivity." The data shows the opposite is slightly more common:
  40.6% of pipelines erode sensitivity (shift toward classifying things as
  healthy), versus 31.2% that erode specificity. And the mean alpha is nearly
  identical between the two threshold-shift groups (-0.013 vs -0.011), meaning
  neither direction of shift is preferentially associated with worse
  performance.

  The truly worst pipelines (general degradation: FP+/FN+, mean α = -0.018) are
  those where the model gets worse at both tasks. This 17.2% group — not the
  specificity-erosion group — represents genuine feature-space damage rather
  than a simple threshold shift.

  Revised hypothesis (H3'): Preprocessing pipelines primarily modulate the CNN's
   implicit decision threshold rather than selectively destroying class-specific
   features. Approximately 72% of preprocessing-induced accuracy changes are
  attributable to threshold shifts (FP and FN anti-correlated), while ~17%
  represent genuine bidirectional feature degradation (FP and FN co-increase).
  The threshold direction depends on how the preprocessing alters the
  distribution of model outputs, with normalization-first sequences tending to
  shift toward the "healthy" classification (sensitivity erosion) and
  equalization-first sequences tending to shift toward "diseased" classification
   (specificity erosion).

  ★ Insight ─────────────────────────────────────
  The r = -0.545 anti-correlation is the key diagnostic. In a
  feature-destruction model, you'd expect FP and FN to be uncorrelated or
  positively correlated (the model gets worse everywhere). In a threshold-shift
  model, they're anti-correlated (one class's errors trade off against the
  other's). The observed -0.545 is strong evidence for threshold shifting as the
   dominant mechanism. However, it's not -1.0, which means there's also a
  non-trivial component of genuine feature-space disruption. The 17.2% of
  pipelines in the FP+/FN+ quadrant would be the interesting ones to investigate
   with Grad-CAM — those are where something is actually breaking, not just
  shifting.

  ├───────────────────┼─────────────┼─────────┼────────────────────────────┤

  ├───────────────────┼─────────────┼─────────┼────────────────────────────┤
  │ Strict monotonic  │ L1 > L2 >   │ p =     │ Borderline — the           │
  │ decline           │ L3 > L4     │ 0.059   │ monotonicity specifically  │
  │                   │             │         │ might be chance            │
  └───────────────────┴─────────────┴─────────┴────────────────────────────┘

  The overall negative association between pipeline length and accuracy gain is 
  not a combinatorial artifact. Even after permuting alpha values across
  pipelines (breaking any real length-dependent structure), only 0.02% of random
   assignments produced a correlation as strong as the observed r = -0.421. The
  combinatorial concern I raised in the critique — that longer pipelines simply
  explore more of the outcome space — does not explain the data.

  However, the strict monotonic decline (every length level worse than the
  previous) falls at p = 0.059, just outside conventional significance. This
  means we can say confidently that longer pipelines tend to be worse, but we
  can't quite confirm that each additional step makes things incrementally worse
   in a perfectly monotonic fashion. The decline from length-2 to length-3, or
  from length-3 to length-4, individually might not be significant — it's the
  overall trend that holds.

  What this means for H5: The qualitative claim stands: longer pipelines are
  worse and no length-4 pipeline is likely to dominate the Pareto frontier. The
  quantitative exponential decay model (P(α > 0) ∝ e^{-λk}) is overfit to 4
  points and should be dropped. The honest claim is: "there is a statistically
  significant negative linear association (r = -0.42, p < 0.001) between
  pipeline length and accuracy gain."

  ★ Insight ─────────────────────────────────────
  Why the permutation test matters: My earlier critique worried that the length
  effect was a combinatorial artifact — longer pipelines have more ways to go
  wrong. The permutation test directly addresses this by asking: "if these 64
  alpha values were randomly assigned to pipeline lengths (maintaining the count
   structure: 4 at length 1, 12 at length 2, etc.), how often would we see a
  correlation this strong?" The answer is: almost never (p = 0.0002). This rules
   out the combinatorial explanation and confirms a genuine length-dependent
  mechanism. The most plausible candidate is error accumulation — each
  additional transform has a probability of degrading the feature space, and
  these probabilities compound.
