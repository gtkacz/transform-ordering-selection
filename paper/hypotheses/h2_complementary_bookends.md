## LaTeX paragraphs — Results section

To investigate whether specific transformations exhibit statistically significant positional preferences within the preprocessing pipeline, the mean $\alpha$ achieved by each transformation was computed as a function of its ordinal position (first through fourth) across all pipelines in which it appears. This positional analysis reveals a striking complementary structure between equalization and normalization. Equalization exhibits a monotonically improving positional gradient, with mean $\alpha$ decreasing from $-0.0052$ when placed first to $-0.0138$ when placed last, yielding a gradient of $+0.0086$ in favor of early placement. Normalization displays the mirror-image pattern, with mean $\alpha$ improving from $-0.0161$ at position one to $-0.0053$ at the final position, yielding a gradient of $-0.0107$ in favor of late placement. To assess whether equalization's first-position advantage reflects a genuine positional effect, pipelines were partitioned into equalization-first ($n = 16$) and equalization-not-first ($n = 48$) groups, revealing a mean $\alpha$ difference of $+0.0083$ in favor of first placement. A permutation test with 100{,}000 iterations established that this advantage is statistically significant ($p = 0.014$), and equalization is the only transformation among the four investigated for which first-position placement achieves significance at the $\alpha < 0.05$ level. Denoising and color space transformation, by contrast, exhibit near-zero positional gradients ($+0.0005$ and $-0.0018$, respectively), confirming that positional sensitivity is not a universal property of preprocessing operations but rather a characteristic of specific transformation types. The complementary positional structure is further confirmed by a bookend analysis examining the joint effect of first and last transformations. Pipelines adopting the equalization-first, normalization-last configuration ($n = 5$) achieve a mean $\alpha$ of $-0.0015$ with 60\% of pipelines yielding positive accuracy gains, compared to a mean $\alpha$ of $-0.0134$ for all other multi-stage pipelines. The inverse configuration — normalization-first, equalization-last — produces a mean $\alpha$ of $-0.0202$ with 0\% positive-$\alpha$ pipelines. A permutation test on the direct contrast between these two bookend configurations yields $p = 0.024$, confirming that the $+0.019$ difference in mean $\alpha$ is statistically significant and that the equalization-normalization ordering is not interchangeable.

## LaTeX paragraphs — Discussion section

The statistically validated positional preferences of equalization and normalization admit a coherent mechanistic interpretation grounded in the mathematical properties of each transformation. Histogram equalization is a nonlinear, data-dependent remapping that redistributes pixel intensities to approximate a uniform cumulative distribution \cite{Pizer1987}. Because this redistribution is computed from the empirical histogram of the input image, its output — and hence the features it enhances — depends critically on the distributional properties of its input. When equalization operates on the raw image (first position), it has access to the full dynamic range and the native intensity distribution, maximizing its capacity to reveal subtle textural contrasts in dermatological lesions. When preceding transformations have already altered the intensity distribution — particularly normalization, which compresses the distribution toward a fixed mean and standard deviation — the histogram from which equalization computes its mapping is informationally impoverished, and the resulting enhancement is correspondingly degraded. Normalization, by contrast, is a linear affine transformation ($x' = (x - \mu) / \sigma$) that is agnostic to the distributional shape of its input; its sole function is to shift and scale pixel values to match the statistical expectations of the downstream convolutional network. This operation is therefore most effective when applied immediately before the network ingestion step, regardless of what transformations precede it. The mirror-image positional gradients observed empirically — equalization improving by $+0.0086$ per position toward the front, normalization improving by $-0.0107$ per position toward the back — are thus direct consequences of these distinct mathematical sensitivities: a distribution-dependent nonlinear operation requires pristine input distributions, while a distribution-agnostic linear standardizer benefits from being the final transform before the network.

This complementary architecture — equalization as opener, normalization as closer — provides a constructive design principle that extends beyond the specific transformations studied here. The bookend analysis demonstrates that the equalization-first, normalization-last sandwich configuration achieves mean performance nearly indistinguishable from the unprocessed baseline ($\overline{\alpha} = -0.0015$) and substantially outperforms the inverse configuration by $+0.019$ ($p = 0.024$), while the catastrophic failure of normalization-first pipelines — which achieve 0\% positive-$\alpha$ rates across all bookend combinations beginning with normalization — reveals that positional misplacement is not merely suboptimal but actively destructive. More broadly, the finding that only two of the four transformations exhibit significant positional sensitivity suggests a general taxonomy of preprocessing operations: \emph{position-sensitive} transforms whose effectiveness depends on input distributional properties (exemplified by equalization) versus \emph{position-neutral} transforms that function comparably regardless of their pipeline placement (exemplified by denoising and color space conversion). For practitioners designing preprocessing pipelines, this taxonomy implies a simple heuristic: position-sensitive, distribution-dependent operations should be placed as early as possible to preserve their access to native image statistics, while linear standardizers should be deferred to the final pipeline stage. Applied to the four-transform case studied here, this heuristic yields the E $\to$ \{D, CS\} $\to$ N template, where the interior operations can be ordered with relative impunity given their positional neutrality, and the boundary operations are fixed by their distributional requirements.
