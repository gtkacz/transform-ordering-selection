## LaTeX paragraphs --- Results section

To quantify the relative contribution of transform selection (which transforms to include in a pipeline) versus transform ordering (how to arrange them), the total variance in $\alpha$ among multi-stage pipelines was decomposed into a between-set component and a within-set component at each pipeline length. Pipelines sharing the same unordered set of transforms were grouped together, and a one-way ANOVA decomposition was computed with transform set as the grouping factor. At pipeline length 2, where each of the six possible transform pairs admits only two orderings, transform selection accounts for the majority of variance in $\alpha$ ($\eta^2 = 0.867$, $F(5,6) = 7.83$, $p = 0.013$), indicating that at this level of complexity the choice of which two transforms to apply is the primary determinant of pipeline performance. This relationship inverts at pipeline length 3, where each of the four possible transform triplets admits six orderings: the within-set (ordering) component now accounts for 77.6\% of total variance ($\eta^2 = 0.224$ for the between-set component, $F(3,20) = 1.92$, $p = 0.158$), meaning that the identity of the transform set no longer significantly predicts $\alpha$ once ordering variation is considered. The same pattern holds under the second experimental condition, where ordering explains 67.2\% of variance at length 3 ($\eta^2 = 0.328$, $F(3,20) = 3.26$, $p = 0.043$); notably, even in this condition where transform selection achieves marginal significance, ordering still accounts for twice as much variance as selection. At pipeline length 4, where all 24 orderings of the complete transform set are evaluated, ordering is the sole source of variance by construction, with a within-set standard deviation of 0.012 and a range in $\alpha$ of 0.058 --- exceeding the entire range of $\alpha$ across all four single-transform pipelines (0.012) by a factor of five. Under the second experimental condition, this ordering-induced range expands to 0.279, representing a 19-fold multiple of the corresponding single-transform selection range.

## LaTeX paragraphs --- Discussion section

The variance decomposition reveals a finding that, while consistent with the mathematical non-commutativity of nonlinear image transformations, is far from trivially predictable: the CNN architecture employed in this study is not ordering-invariant. A sufficiently powerful feature extractor could, in principle, learn representations that abstract away the differences between alternative orderings of the same transform set, rendering ordering irrelevant to classification accuracy. The empirical result --- that ordering accounts for 68--78\% of variance in $\alpha$ at pipeline length 3 and for 100\% at length 4 --- demonstrates that the four-block convolutional architecture used here lacks this invariance property, and that the feature-space distortions introduced by different orderings are large enough to propagate through the entire network to the classification layer. The crossover from selection-dominated variance at length 2 to ordering-dominated variance at length 3 admits a combinatorial interpretation: with two transforms, there are only two possible orderings per set, limiting the scope for ordering-induced divergence; with three transforms, there are six orderings, and with four there are 24, providing increasingly many opportunities for detrimental arrangements to emerge. This combinatorial argument does not, by itself, explain why the mean $\alpha$ declines with length --- it explains only why the variance increases --- but it does identify ordering proliferation as the mechanism through which longer pipelines explore larger regions of the performance space, with the majority of those regions corresponding to suboptimal configurations.

The practical implication of ordering dominance is that the conventional approach to preprocessing pipeline design --- selecting a set of beneficial transforms and applying them in a conventionally motivated order --- is insufficient for pipelines of length three or greater. At these lengths, the choice of arrangement is the primary determinant of whether a pipeline improves or degrades classification accuracy, and evaluating a single ordering of a promising transform set provides almost no information about the performance of alternative orderings of the same set. The mean within-set range in $\alpha$ grows from 0.005 at pipeline length 2, where a single swap produces only modest differences, to 0.029 at length 3 and 0.058 at length 4, where rearranging the same four transforms can swing accuracy by nearly six percentage points. This growth in ordering sensitivity is not uniform across transform sets: at pipeline length 3, the within-set range varies from 0.012 for sets containing only positionally neutral transforms (denoising and color space conversion) to 0.042 for sets containing both equalization and normalization, the two transforms identified elsewhere in this analysis as exhibiting strong and opposing positional preferences. The heterogeneity in ordering sensitivity thus maps directly onto the taxonomy of position-sensitive versus position-neutral transforms, providing convergent evidence that the ordering effect is driven by specific distributional dependencies rather than by a generic property of sequential image processing.
